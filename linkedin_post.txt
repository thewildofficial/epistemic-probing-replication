Just ran a replication study that I want to share — and it comes with a puzzle I can't stop thinking about.

The paper "No Answer Needed" by Iván Vicente Moreno Cencerrado, Arnau Padrés Masdemont, Anton Gonzalvez Hawthorne, David Demitri Africa, and Lorenzo Pacchiardi showed something wild: you can predict whether an LLM will answer correctly BEFORE it generates a single token. How? Train a linear probe on the model's intermediate activations — the hidden states from the middle layers — right after it reads the question but before it starts answering.

The model internally encodes whether it's about to be right or wrong. And you can read that out with a simple linear classifier. No output needed. Just the question-time activations.

I replicated this on Qwen3.5-4B (a different architecture than their Llama-based experiments). 656 questions — 456 MMLU factual QA + 200 GSM8K math reasoning. Results:

✅ Factual QA (MMLU): AUROC 0.87 — the probe can almost always tell if the model's about to be right or wrong. And here's the kicker: a training-free probe (literally just taking the difference between mean-correct and mean-wrong activations, zero parameters) hits 0.88. The "correctness direction" is just sitting there in the geometry of the activation space. You don't even need to train anything to find it.

❌ Reasoning (GSM8K): AUROC 0.64 — barely above a coin flip. The same signal that works cleanly for factual recall just doesn't work for mathematical reasoning.

This is the same wall the original paper hit. The reasoning gap is real and it replicates across model families.

So what does this mean?

The model has a form of internal self-monitoring for factual knowledge. It "knows" when it's on solid ground vs when it's guessing — before it says anything. But this monitoring system doesn't extend to reasoning. Either the uncertainty is encoded differently (maybe across token positions, not just the last one), or the model hasn't even started the reasoning computation at prefill time, or reasoning correctness is just fundamentally harder to linearly separate.

This is a live research project — next steps are multi-position probing (extract from all token positions, not just the last), testing whether RLHF destroys this signal (base vs aligned model comparison), and eventually using the correctness direction to STEER the model's strategy when it detects uncertainty.
